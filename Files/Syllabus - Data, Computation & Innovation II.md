## **About This Course**

The goal of this course is to become familiar with the tooling for JavaScript-based visualization development, and to leave knowing enough to keep learning in whatever direction your work takes you.

You will not become a JavaScript expert, but you'll understand enough to build real things, adapt other people's code, and recognize what you don't know. You will not become a D3 master, but you'll understand what D3 is actually doing and be able to use it as a tool rather than a black box.

What you will leave with: a working mental model of how JavaScript-based interactive graphics are built, hands-on experience with the tools used in modern newsrooms, and at least one portfolio-worthy project you made yourself.

We'll work primarily in Svelte and D3. We'll cover interactivity, animation, mapping, scrollytelling, and how to get your work live on the internet and embedded.

This is a making class. Most of what you learn will come from building things, breaking them, and building them again.

## **On Using AI**

AI tools are part of the industry and ignoring them would be a disservice. That said, this course exists to teach you how things work. Using AI to complete assignments you haven't yet learned to do yourself defeats the purpose.

The expected use of AI in this course is as a learning aid: to explain code you don't understand, to help debug a specific error, or to explore an approach before implementing it yourself. It is not a substitute for doing the work.

For the final project, you are expected to disclose AI assistance in your process document – what you used it for, what it gave you, and what you changed. The same goes for code adapted from examples in the wild. Being precise about this is a professional skill. Submitting AI-generated or copied work without acknowledgment is a violation of the school's code of conduct.

## **Assignments**

### **Weekly Homework**

Weekly assignments are short and frequent. They are graded on completion. An assignment is complete when it meets the basic requirements; work that is cursory or clearly rushed is not. The goal is repetition and iteration. You will learn more from doing ten imperfect things than from agonizing over one.

Every week you can expect homework assignments that will include all or a combination of:

* The completion of a section of a MOOC or other technical tutorial  
* One or more chart deliverables  
* Contributing to the weekly chart discussion thread on CourseWorks  
* A final project update

Weekly homework assignments are to be completed and if applicable submitted prior to the start of the next class.

### **Final Project**

The final project is a portfolio-worthy interactive data visualization built across the semester. It will be evaluated on editorial clarity, technical execution, and design decisions. The accompanying process document describes your data sources, the key decisions you made, and what you built from scratch versus adapted from existing examples or generated with AI assistance.

#### **Requirements**

The project must tell a clear journalistic story grounded in real data. It should be built in Svelte, published to the web (GitHub Pages or equivalent), and include at least one interactive element that serves the story rather than decorating it. The final piece should be something you would show in a portfolio or a job interview.

The project should be able to stand on its own. A reader who arrives without context should be able to understand what they're looking at, why it matters, and what to take away. That might mean a data story with charts woven into a narrative, a visual essay where the visuals carry the argument, or a dashboard with enough framing and annotation to guide the reader without requiring explanation. Whatever the form, the editorial thinking should be as visible as the technical work.

#### **Process document**

Submit a short written document alongside your final project. It should cover: 

* what story you set out to tell and whether that changed  
* the data source and any cleaning or transformation you did  
* the key design decisions you made and why  
* a plain accounting of what you wrote yourself, what you adapted from examples or documentation, and where you used AI assistance.

#### **Presentations**

Week 7 is dedicated to final presentations. Each student presents for five to ten minutes, followed by a few minutes of questions and a group critique. You should be prepared to walk through the story, explain at least one technical or design decision, and share something that didn't work the way you expected. As this is intended to be a portfolio item, students are encouraged to take the feedback from myself and peers and further refine their project.

#### **Milestones**

The project thread runs across the course with lightweight checkpoints at each class session. 

* **Week 3 – Pitch:** One paragraph describing the story you want to tell, the data source you plan to use, and why it benefits from a visual treatment.  
* **Week 4 – Data in hand:** Have your dataset acquired and at least partially cleaned. Prepare a rough sketch (on paper or in code) of the visual structure you're considering.  
* **Week 5 – Narrative outline:** Take your sketch from Week 4 and build the story around it. What does the reader need to understand, and in what order? How does the visual fit into that structure?  
* **Week 6 – Task list:** Break the remaining work into GitHub Issues in your project repository. What still needs to get built? What's unresolved? What are you unsure about? The issues don't need to be perfectly scoped. The goal is to practice thinking in tasks.

## **Critique**

Most classes will open with small-group critique of the previous week's work. This peer review is modeled on how many graphics desks actually operate and in my opinion is one of the more valuable parts of the course. You get good at data viz by looking at a lot of data viz.

Small groups will rotate through each week. The format is simple: what's working, what's unclear, ask specific questions, give specific suggestions. Critique meets work where it is. Early-stage feedback should focus on direction and clarity of intent; later-stage feedback on execution and whether the piece is landing. What you say about a rough prototype should be different from what you say about something nearly finished.

Learning to edit is one of the most valuable skills you can develop, and it applies as much to your own work as to your peers'. The best visualizations are almost always the product of cuts – a layer removed, a label simplified, an interaction stripped back. That process can be uncomfortable, especially when you've spent time building something. But the discomfort is part of it. Knowing what to take out, and being willing to take it out, is what separates work that's technically impressive from work that actually lands.  
Come prepared to give feedback that's honest and specific, and to receive it the same way.
